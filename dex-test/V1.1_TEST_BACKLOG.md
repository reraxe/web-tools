# Dex v1.1-test Backlog

This file tracks issues discovered while testing `v1.0-stable`. Changes are implemented and verified in `v1.1-test` without modifying the stable deployment or its SQLite data.

## Release scope

`v1.1-test` is the first consolidated quality-of-life release. It includes the work previously considered for `v1.1-test` through `v1.4-test`: essential fixes, multi-card batch organization, faster intake, safer daily controls, and complete exports.

The release must make manual inventory organization efficient and dependable. Automatic card recognition, synchronized card catalogs, marketplace intelligence, and additional game adapters begin with `v2.0-test`.

## 1. Camera QR scanning requires HTTPS

**Observed:** The phone displays: "Camera QR scanning needs HTTPS on this device. You can type the SKU for now."

**Cause:** Mobile browsers block camera access when Dex is opened over unsecured HTTP.

**Target:**

- Publish Dex through the intranet reverse proxy at `https://dex.home`.
- Use a certificate trusted by the phone.
- Keep manual SKU entry available as a fallback.
- Improve the UI message so it clearly distinguishes missing HTTPS from unsupported QR detection.

**Acceptance:** The phone can open the rear camera, scan a Dex QR label, and add the SKU to an outbound order.

## 2. Text must fit the 2 x 1 thermal label

**Observed:** The SKU and description area is cramped and can extend beyond its intended label field.

**Target:**

- Keep the QR code on the left and SKU/details on the right.
- Dynamically size or wrap long SKU text inside the available width.
- Keep the game and set description within the label boundary.
- Preserve exact 2 x 1 inch print dimensions without clipping.

**Acceptance:** The longest supported SKU and its description print completely inside a 2 x 1 label and remain readable on the iDPRT SP310.

## 3. Batch and SKU dates must use the business timezone

**Observed:** A batch created during the evening of June 20 received a June 21 date because the server uses UTC.

**Target:**

- Add a configurable timezone with `America/New_York` as the deployment setting.
- Use that timezone for batch codes, SKUs, sale dates, and displayed timestamps.

**Acceptance:** A batch created before local midnight uses the correct local calendar date regardless of the server's system timezone.

## 4. Bulk scan intake and image pairing

**Observed:** Browser intake requires adding one front/back pair at a time.

**Target:**

- Accept an entire batch of front/back images in one drag-and-drop action.
- Pair files automatically using front/back filenames when available, with sequential pairing as a fallback.
- Flag incomplete or ambiguous filename/order pairs in the review grid and provide a one-click front/back swap control.
- Assign a unique SKU to every physical card immediately.
- Apply the batch's game, set, color, condition, finish group, and location automatically.
- Show batch progress and allow pair correction before completion.
- Keep manual identification available for each card in the batch grid.

**Acceptance:** A complete scan batch can be dropped into Dex once, paired into physical-card entries, assigned unique SKUs, and efficiently identified from the batch grid.

## 5. Add cards to a completed batch

**Observed:** After a batch is finished, its intake form is hidden and there is no way to add cards that were missed or found later.

**Target:**

- Show an **Add more cards** button in the top-right batch status area when a batch is complete.
- Reopen the same batch and restore browser upload and watched-folder intake.
- Preserve every existing card, image, SKU, cost allocation, and printed-label status.
- Continue the SKU sequence for newly added physical cards.
- When the batch is finished again, queue labels only for new or otherwise unprinted cards.
- Record when the batch was reopened and completed again for audit history.

**Acceptance:** A completed batch can be reopened, receive additional cards, and be completed again without changing existing SKUs or reprinting old labels.

## 6. Completed batches are incorrectly labeled Sold

**Observed:** The inbound batch list displays a completed batch as **Sold**, while opening the batch correctly shows its card as **In stock**.

**Cause:** The batch list reuses card-status badges and incorrectly maps every non-open batch to `SOLD`.

**Target:**

- Use distinct batch statuses: **Open**, **Complete**, and later **Reopened**.
- Reserve **Sold** exclusively for individual cards processed through outbound.
- Optionally summarize available and sold card counts separately within each batch row.

**Acceptance:** Finishing intake changes the batch badge to **Complete** without changing or misrepresenting the inventory status of any card.

## 7. Batch workspace must support and display many cards

**Observed:** Although `v1.0-stable` can accept multiple cards before completion, intake is presented as a one-card-at-a-time form and completed batches do not provide a useful visual overview of all scanned cards.

**Target:**

- Treat every new batch as a multi-card intake workspace.
- Add all browser-uploaded or watched-folder scan pairs as separate physical-card entries in that batch.
- Display batch contents as a responsive grid with the card image, name, card number, SKU, rarity, variant, and review status.
- Keep duplicate physical copies as separate entries with their own SKUs.
- Allow sorting and filtering by scan order, card number, rarity, and needs-review status.
- Open an individual card from the grid to correct identification, swap front/back, or edit inventory details.
- Show batch totals for scanned, identified, needs review, and labels waiting.
- Preserve the grid after the batch is completed or reopened.

**Acceptance:** Opening a batch containing multiple scans shows every physical card in a clear grid, and each card can be reviewed or edited without leaving the batch workflow.

## v1.1a-test: Bulk scan front/back orientation

**Observed:** A valid front/back pair can display the card back as the batch thumbnail. Generic scanner filenames do not identify sides, and Dex currently assumes the alphabetically first image is the front.

**Confirmed fi-7160 behavior:** With the physical card face oriented up/outward, PaperStream Capture produced the card back as page 1 (`image1803.png`) and the card face as page 2 (`image1804.png`). This workflow therefore requires **Back first** sequential pairing unless explicit `_front` and `_back` filenames are supplied.

With the physical card facing down, PaperStream saves the card face first and the card back second. The face-down scan also produced the better image-quality result in testing, so **Face down / Front first** is the recommended fi-7160 workflow for Dex.

**Target:**

- Show both images in the batch review card before completion.
- Add a per-card **Swap front/back** command that exchanges the stored image roles without changing the SKU or inventory record.
- Add a batch intake setting for **Front first** or **Back first**, with Front first as the default.
- Apply the selected scanner order consistently to sequential browser uploads and watched-folder intake.
- Preserve explicit `_front` and `_back` filename pairing regardless of the batch order setting.
- Show the paired filenames and orientation in the upload review so the user can catch mistakes before saving a large batch.

**Acceptance:** A back-first scanner batch can be imported with the correct card faces as thumbnails, and any incorrectly oriented existing card can be corrected without deleting or re-creating its SKU.

## Deferred to v2.3-test: DPS (Dex Pre-grading System)

- Build an internal card-condition pre-screen named **DPS: Dex Pre-grading System**, not an official PSA, Beckett, TAG, or other grading-company grade.
- Measure front/back centering ratios, border geometry, corner shape, edge whitening/chipping, print defects, and visible surface anomalies from high-resolution scans.
- Generate grayscale, contrast, edge, emboss-style, and difference-map inspection views while retaining the untouched source images.
- Treat emboss-style filters only as visual evidence; do not interpret a 2D filter as physical depth measurement.
- Support controlled raking-light or multi-angle photographs for scratches, dents, indentations, gloss defects, foil print lines, texture, and warping that a flatbed scan may miss.
- Produce transparent centering, corners, edges, surface, and print-quality sub-scores with marked evidence regions and confidence levels.
- Report a conservative grade range and **Needs human inspection** findings instead of promising a precise or guaranteed grade.
- Maintain separate configurable comparison profiles based on currently published grading criteria after licensing and use restrictions are verified.
- Avoid branding the output as a certified PSA/BGS/TAG result or implying affiliation with those companies.
- Validate predictions against a growing local set of cards that were subsequently graded, tracking accuracy and systematic bias by card type and finish.
- Add grading-submission ROI estimates only after marketplace pricing is available, including grading fees, shipping, expected value by grade, and uncertainty.

### DPS differentiators

- Publish an evidence-first measurement report with exact centering ratios, marked corner/edge/surface regions, defect coordinates, severity, and the source image used for every finding.
- Keep the DPS numerical condition score separate from estimated third-party outcomes so measurements remain stable when an external grading profile changes.
- Version every scoring model and threshold so the same card can be reproduced and audited against the exact DPS release that evaluated it.
- Score capture quality before grading and refuse to force a result when focus, glare, clipping, resolution, alignment, or missing lighting angles make the evidence unreliable.
- Report calibrated uncertainty, likely range, limiting defect, and **Needs human inspection** rather than hiding ambiguity behind a single number.
- Provide a repeatability test that compares multiple captures of the same card and warns when the estimated result is unstable.
- Support straight-on flatbed scans plus optional raking-light, cross-polarized, and multi-angle captures for surface evidence.
- Track condition changes over time by comparing later scans with the card's original DPS evidence map.
- Connect pre-grading directly to the physical SKU, inventory history, marketplace value, grading cost, submission status, and eventual returned grade.
- Build an unbiased feedback loop from returned grades without silently changing earlier DPS reports.

### DPS validation plan

- Use scans captured before submission, then link the returned professional grade; avoid training from slab scans when plastic, labels, or known grades could leak into the prediction.
- Begin engineering with 30-50 cards covering obvious defects and clean examples.
- Run a first blind pilot with at least 200-300 cards balanced across grade bands, games, finishes, border styles, eras, and defect types.
- Require at least 385 independent holdout cards for an overall accuracy estimate near a 95% confidence level with roughly a plus/minus 5 percentage-point margin under conservative assumptions.
- Target 500-1,000 cards for a credible first public-quality validation, with at least 50 and preferably 100 examples in every important grade band or card subgroup being reported separately.
- Keep training, calibration, and final holdout cards strictly separated by physical card; duplicate scans of one card must never appear across those groups.
- Measure exact-grade agreement, within-one-grade agreement, mean absolute error, overgrade/undergrade bias, confusion matrices, range coverage, confidence calibration, and repeat-scan stability.
- Treat the returned professional grade as a reference outcome rather than perfect ground truth; use multiple graders, crossover results, or resubmissions where practical to quantify grader disagreement.

**Reality boundary:** Straight-on flatbed scans are strong for centering, corners, edges, and obvious print/surface defects. They cannot by themselves deliver near-100% grading accuracy because some defects require depth, changing illumination, magnification, tactile inspection, or proprietary/human judgment.
